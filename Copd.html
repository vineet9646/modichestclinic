Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a chronic lung disease that mainly results in an obstructed airflow from the lungs. It is frequently caused by long-term exposure to various gases or smoking. Patients who have COPD are at a higher risk of developing heart, lung, and various disorders.
Chronic bronchitis and Emphysema are the two most common conditions that contribute to COPD. Both the diseases may occur together and vary in severity amongst individuals.
Emphysema is a disease in which the air sacs (alveoli) at the end of the smallest air passages of the lungs are destroyed.
Chronic bronchitis is a disease where damage to the lining of the bronchial tubes occurs. These tubes carry air to and from the air sacs (alveoli) of the lungs
Let’s understand more about this condition!

  • Causes

    A primary cause of chronic bronchitis is usually long-term exposure to irritants that damage the lungs and airways.
    Smoking cigarettes, pipes, cigars, and other types of tobacco smoke can also cause chronic bronchitis.
    The other irritants include air pollution, secondhand smoke, chemical fumes, and dust from the environment or workplace.
    Very rarely, a genetic condition can play a role in causing chronic bronchitis.
    The risk factors of chronic bronchitis include the following:
    • Age
    • Genetics
    • Long-term exposure to irritant gases or lung irritants
    • Smoking

  • Symptoms

    Initially, you may have no symptoms or mild symptoms only. However, as the disease gets worse, your symptoms are likely to become more severe. The symptoms include:
    • Wheezing (a whistling sound while breathing)
    • Coughing that produces a lot of mucus
    • Tightness in chest
    • Breathlessness, especially with physical activity
    Some people may get frequent respiratory infections such as cold and flu. In severe cases, chronic bronchitis may cause weakness in your lower muscles, swelling in ankles, feet, legs, and weight loss.

  • Treatment

    Various treatment options are available to manage the symptoms, slow the progress of the disease, and improve your ability to stay active. Some common options include:
    • Lifestyle changes: If you are a smoker, you must quit smoking; this is the most crucial step that one has to take to treat chronic bronchitis. It would be best to avoid places that produce secondhand gases or lung irritants. Improve physical activity and strengthen the muscles that may help you breathe and improve your overall wellness.
    • Oxygen rehabilitation: Severe chronic bronchitis may cause low oxygen levels in your blood. In such cases, oxygen therapy can help you to breathe better.
    • Medications: Drugs that relax your airways (bronchodilators) may be prescribed to open your airways and make breathing easier. Most of them are to be taken through an inhaler. In severe cases, steroids may also be advised to reduce inflammation.
    Antibiotics may help if you get bacterial infections or a viral lung infection.
    Vaccines for flu and pneumococcal pneumonia are administered as people with chronic bronchitis are at higher risk of severe problems from these diseases.
    • Pulmonary Rehabilitation: This program helps in improving the well-being of people who have chronic breathing issues.
    It may include the following:
    • Psychological counseling
    • Nutritional counseling
    • Exercise program
    • Disease management training
    • Lung Transplant: A lung transplant may be considered for people with severe symptoms where none of the abovementioned treatments provide relief.
